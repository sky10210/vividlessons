Credit Scores 101

A plain teacher-facing guide for the Credit Scores 101 interactive module. This guide is not a duplicate of the student module. It is designed for planning, answer checking, reflection review, and classroom use.

Overview

Module Purpose

Students learn what a credit score is, why it matters, and how everyday financial behavior can strengthen or damage credit over time.

Best Use Cases

  • Personal finance credit unit
  • Business basics money decision activity
  • Sub plan or independent work
  • Credit cards, loans, or borrowing introduction
  • Review activity before a credit or banking assessment
Learning Goals

Students Should Be Able To

  • Explain what a credit score is.
  • Identify why credit scores matter.
  • Recognize the role of payment history.
  • Understand basic credit utilization.
  • Separate smart and risky credit behaviors.
  • Reflect on one habit that can protect credit.

Student Fields Collected

First name, last name, class period, answers, score, completion status, timing, and reflection.

Answer Key

Question-by-Question Guide

Question 1 — Credit Score Definition Correct answer should identify a credit score as a number lenders use to estimate borrowing risk. It is not the same as income, bank balance, or total wealth.
Question 2 — Why Credit Scores Matter Correct answer should connect stronger credit to possible approval advantages, lower interest rates, better loan terms, apartment applications, or lower borrowing costs.
Question 3 — Payment History Correct answer: Paying bills on time is one of the most important credit habits. Late or missed payments can hurt credit.
Question 4 — Credit Utilization Correct answer should show that using a lower percentage of available credit is generally better than maxing out a card.
Question 5 — Credit Cards and Loans Correct answer should recognize that both credit cards and loans can affect credit, depending on how they are used and repaid.
Question 6 — Matching Credit Factors Expected matches should connect payment history with on-time payments, utilization with balance compared to credit limit, credit mix with types of credit, and new credit with recent applications/accounts.
Question 7 — Sorting Smart Credit Steps Smart credit steps should include paying on time, keeping balances low, checking statements, borrowing only what can be repaid, and avoiding unnecessary applications. Risky steps include missing payments, maxing out cards, ignoring bills, or opening too many accounts quickly.
Question 8 — Credit Utilization Calculation Correct answer should calculate utilization as balance divided by credit limit. Example: $300 balance ÷ $1,000 limit = 30% utilization.
Question 9 — Final Credit Decision Correct answer should choose the behavior that protects or improves credit over time, usually the option involving on-time payment, lower balance, and responsible borrowing.
Reflection

Reflection Guidance

Student answers will vary. Strong responses should include a realistic credit habit, a reason the habit matters, and a personal or real-world connection.

Look For

  • Mentions paying on time.
  • Mentions keeping card balances low.
  • Explains why borrowing decisions matter.
  • Uses a realistic example.
  • Shows understanding that credit affects future opportunities.
Teacher Notes

Suggested Use

  • Use as a 15–30 minute independent lesson.
  • Use after introducing credit cards, loans, or borrowing.
  • Have students screenshot, print, or submit the certificate if needed.
  • Review reflections as a quick exit ticket.
  • Use completion data to check who finished.

Common Misunderstandings

  • Students may confuse income with credit score.
  • Students may think having no debt always means having strong credit.
  • Students may not understand that late payments can have lasting effects.
